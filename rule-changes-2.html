George Mikan

George Mikan, nicknamed "Mr. Basketball," was one of the most influential players in the early days of professional basketball. His dominance and unique playing style led to several significant rule changes in the sport.

The Goaltending Rule:
Mikan's ability to swat away shots near the rim led to the introduction of the goaltending rule in college basketball in 1944.
The "Mikan Rule":
The NBA widened the lane from 6 to 12 feet in 1951 to reduce Mikan's dominance near the basket.
The Shot Clock:
Mikan's offensive prowess indirectly led to the introduction of the 24-second shot clock in the 1954-55 season.

Paul George suffered a severe leg injury during a Team USA scrimmage on August 1, 2014, while preparing for the FIBA World Cup. The injury occurred when George's leg collided with the basket stanchion as he attempted to block a fast-break layup.

Following this incident, the NBA did indeed make changes to improve player safety:

Increased stanchion distance:
The league mandated that the basket stanchion be placed at least 4 feet from the baseline, increasing the space between players and the structure.
Padding requirements:
Additional padding was required around the stanchion to provide extra protection in case of collisions.
Photographer positioning:
The league also implemented stricter rules on where photographers and cameramen could be positioned during games to create more open space near the baskets.

Micheal Jordan

Michael Jordan's play and impact on the game influenced the creation or modification of three significant NBA rules:

The Flagrant Foul Rule:
This rule was implemented to protect players from excessively hard fouls. Jordan's aggressive style of play and his ability to drive to the basket often resulted in hard fouls from defenders trying to stop him. The league introduced the flagrant foul rule to discourage overly physical play and protect offensive players.
Hand Checking Rule:
The NBA banned hand checking in 1994, with further restrictions added in 2004. This rule change was partly in response to the physical defense used against Jordan. Defenders would often use their hands to impede offensive players' movement, particularly against elite scorers like Jordan. The removal of hand checking made it easier for perimeter players to drive to the basket and score.
Max Contract Rule:
While not directly related to on-court play, the max contract rule was influenced by Jordan's enormous salary in his final years with the Chicago Bulls. In the 1997-98 season, Jordan's salary alone exceeded the entire salary cap. This led the NBA to implement a maximum salary rule in the 1999 Collective Bargaining Agreement to prevent single players from taking up such a large portion of a team's salary cap.

These rule changes were not solely because of Jordan, but his dominance and impact on the game certainly played a significant role in their implementation. The changes aimed to improve player safety, increase scoring, and maintain competitive balance in the league.

Zaza Pachulia

Zaza Pachulia's actions led to two rule changes in the NBA, often referred to as "Zaza rules":

The All-Star Voting Rule:
In 2017, Zaza Pachulia nearly made the All-Star team due to an unusual surge in fan votes from his home country of Georgia, despite his modest stats. In response, the NBA changed the All-Star voting system, making fan votes worth 50% less. This gave the NBA more control over the selection process to ensure that players are chosen based on merit rather than popularity or coordinated voting campaigns.
The Closeout Rule:
During Game 1 of the 2017 Western Conference Finals, Pachulia's closeout on Kawhi Leonard caused Leonard to land on Pachulia's foot, aggravating an ankle injury. This incident was widely criticized as a dangerous play. As a result, the NBA implemented a rule for the 2017-18 season allowing referees to call flagrant or technical fouls on reckless closeouts. Under this rule, defenders must allow space for a shooter to land safely after a jump shot.

These rule changes were implemented to improve player safety and maintain the integrity of the All-Star selection process. The closeout rule, in particular, aims to prevent injuries resulting from aggressive defensive tactics on jump shooters.
